His Story

A Michigan Story

Growing Up in the Upper Peninsula

Justin Kasieta grew up in a small town on Michigan's Upper Peninsula. His childhood was like that of so many Michigan families вЂ” hardworking, close-knit, grounded in faith and community.

But when Justin was 15, everything changed. His father was diagnosed with aggressive cancer. The disease moved fast. Justin's dad passed away when Justin was just entering his teenage years.

Becoming the Caretaker

At 13 years old, Justin became the caretaker for his four younger siblings. He walked to the store to buy food for them. The family survived on Social Security survivor benefits вЂ” the safety net his father had paid into his entire working life.

Then Justin entered Michigan's foster care system. He lived in four different homes. He attended four different high schools. He was alone, bouncing between strangers, trying to finish school while grieving his father and worrying about his siblings.

"At 13, I walked to the store to buy food for my four younger siblings. I know what it means to fight for your family."

The Betrayal

Years later, Justin discovered something that changed his life: the State of Michigan had taken over $18,000 in Social Security survivor benefits вЂ” money that was supposed to help him and his siblings after their father died вЂ” and used it to reimburse the cost of his foster care.

They never told him. They never told his family. They just took it.

Justin learned that this wasn't just happening to him. This practice was affecting more than 25,000 foster children nationally, with states seizing over $174 million per year in benefits meant for orphaned and disabled children.

"It's not fair to make kids who are orphaned or disabled have to pay for their own foster care. But kids who are not orphaned or disabled receive taxpayer dollars to cover their foster care?" вЂ” Justin Kasieta, testimony before Michigan Legislature

Fighting Back

Justin didn't just accept it. He challenged the system. He became a national advocate for foster children's rights.

He testified before the Michigan Legislature in support of Senate Bill 18 to protect foster children's benefits. He interned in the Michigan state legislature and the U.S. Congress. He shared his story on NPR, CBS News, MSN, and GPB News.

And it worked. Fifteen states reformed their policies thanks to advocacy like his. The Trump administration issued warnings to states about this practice. Real change happened.

The Success Story

Despite everything вЂ” losing his father, becoming a caretaker at 13, entering foster care, being moved four times, attending four different high schools, having his benefits stolen вЂ” Justin graduated from the University of Michigan on scholarships.

He built a career in finance and government. He became a voice for the voiceless. He proved that foster children can succeed when given a fighting chance.

"If the goal is to make success stories like mine the rule, instead of the exception, I think we need to support kids all the way through from the time they are in foster care to the time they exit and even beyond that." вЂ” Justin Kasieta

Why He's Running

Justin knows what it's like when government fails you. He knows what it's like to be overlooked, underestimated, and forgotten. He knows what it means to fight for your family when the system is stacked against you.

Now, he's ready to take that fight to Washington вЂ” to protect Michigan's children, strengthen our families, and ensure government works for the people, not against them.
